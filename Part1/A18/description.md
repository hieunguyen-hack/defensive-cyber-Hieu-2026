Case 1: Microsoft Tay (Twitter, 2016):

What happened:
Microsoft released “Tay,” a conversational AI chatbot on Twitter designed to learn language patterns from real-time interactions with users, especially targeting casual, teenage-style communication.

Attack:
Within hours, coordinated users (including trolls) began exploiting Tay by sending large volumes of malicious inputs, including:
Racist and extremist statements
Hate speech and conspiracy content
Repetitive phrases to reinforce patterns (“repeat after me” attacks)

Result:
Tay started autonomously generating offensive, racist, and politically extreme tweets, mimicking the harmful content it had learned.
Some outputs included explicit hate speech and denial of historical events.

Cause (technical perspective):
Online learning without validation: Tay updated its responses directly from user input
Lack of content filtering/moderation layer
No adversarial input handling (system assumed users were benign)
Over-reliance on unsupervised learning from untrusted data sources

Source: https://www.bbc.com/news/technology-35902104

Case 2: Google AI Overviews: "Glue on Pizza, eat a rock per day":

Google’s AI Overviews generated bizarre and unsafe misinformation, showing that generative AI can struggle to distinguish reliable facts from unreliable internet content.

Cause:
AI doesn’t understand truth — only patterns/popularity
  It selects information that appears frequently online, not what is correct
It can mistake jokes or satire for facts
  e.g., Reddit posts or satirical articles being treated as real advice
Trained on the open web, so it inherits:
  biases, misinformation, and low-quality content'
